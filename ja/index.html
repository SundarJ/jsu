日本語のリンク

Introduction

So, here's some basic things about Japanese (日本語) that I think might help!

First of all, "Japan" in Japanese is 日本, which comprises the Chinese characters "sun/day" and "origin" (which also happens to mean "book"). This is because Japan imported the Chinese characters from China thousands of years ago, and, from the viewpoint of China, Japan is in the direction of the rising sun.

Alphabet

There are 3 alphabets in Japanese - Hiragana, Katakana, and Kanji:

ひらがな is used for native Japanese words, such as くるま (car). Little kids use it before they've learnt how to write the kanji for the words (that's why Sailor Moon was teased about it, if you've seen that). You can write all Japanese words in this, although it's harder to read than Kanji when everything is written in it, since there are lots of Homophones in Japanese, so it would be hard to tell which word meant what - also, it ends up looking like a garbled mess of characters, like if you removed the spaces from an English paragraph.

Compare: わたしはさんだーともうします。ねこがすきんですが、ねことあそびたい with this: 私はサンダーと申します。猫が好きんですが、猫と遊びたい。

Katakana is used mainly for foreign loan words, like サンキュー・ベリー・マッチ, but is also used often for animal names, like ネコ. It's also often used on Twitter and such in place of italics (for emphasis), since it stands out from hiragana.

Kanji is what most things are written in. It came from China, and represents words as ideographs - some words look like what they mean, for example 木 (tree), whereas others... don't. Such as, 図書館 (library). There are 2136 Kanji that the Japanese government designates as common use, and that all high school students learn. Don't worry, though, only about half of them are in super common use, but all are a must to know.

Grammar

The Grammar is one of the most difficult, but interesting parts of Japanese. As with lots of things in Japanese, it's backwards to the way it's done in English. English is a SVO (Subject-Verb-Object) language, which means that sentences are phrased like so: "John (subject) eats (verb) the apple (object)". Japanese, on the other hand, is an SOV, language - the object and verb are swapped. The previous sentence would look like this: "John the apple eats". In Japanese: ジョン は アップル を 食べる。

The previous sentence introduces you to a fundamental part of Japanese grammar: particles (助詞. They are what separates the different grammatical parts of the sentence. In the previous one, は is the "topic marker" - it tells you what the sentence is about (John), and を, which is what attaches the verb to the object. In casual Japanese (aka anime), these are often left out.

There are also different formality levels in speech in Japanese - you'll say different words, or conjugate things differently depending on whether you're talking to friends, or someone of authority. This isn't too too different from English (you wouldn't say "mate" to a teacher, right?), but Japanese takes it a step or two further. Just keep that in mind.

Anyway, now on to the resources. I recommend you learn both kanas first before moving onto any other Japanese learning — they're the ABCs of Japanese

    • k»g (ka becomes ga)
    • ts»dz (tsu becomes dzu, pronounced zu)
    • t»d (te becomes de)
    • h»b (ho becomes bo)
    • ch/sh» (shi and chi become ji)

    with a handakuten (は»ぱ), h becomes p (ha becomes pa)

    small ゃゅょ: added to kana ending in -i, truncates sound (きゃ»kya, not kiya)

    small ぃ: used to form sounds foreign to Japanese, such as ふぃ, fi.

    small っ: indicates that the following consonant is doubled—さか:saka, さっか:sakka
